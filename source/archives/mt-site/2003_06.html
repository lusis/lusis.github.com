War Cribbage

Michelle and I just finished a rousing game of "War Cribbage"

It’s fun too! Basically it started out one night when I whupped up on Michelle after dinner. I think it was my first double skunk. We started messing around with the cards and playing cribbage in the same style as war.

Tonight we refined the game to it's ultimate version - A DRINKING GAME!

Here's how it works. Each person needs a deck. For Christmas, along with my new Cribbage Board, I got a NICE twin pack of Kem’s.

You reset your pegs to start. Each person takes a deck and you start. It's not as fast paced as war but it's just as fun.

- Take turns flipping cards - War Style.
- Any points scored during play (15s, runs, pairs), forces the looser to drink while the winner counts out the number of points.
e.g. Michelle plays 6, I play 7, she plays an 8. I now have to drink while she counts to 3. She doesn’t want to count TOO slow because if I lay down a 5, it’s going to be the longest 4 seconds she's ever known ;)
- 31 only counts for a point and only a quick drink is required. That also gives you a point on the board.
- A bust ( going over 31 ) is also a point. As before, looser only has to take a quick swig on a single point.
- After each hand (31 points or bust), the cards are moved off to your own side in case you need to recycle.
- Nobs are a bit unique. It works this way:
Nobs are only awarded when a jack is played that matches the suit of the previously played card. Since each person has their own desk, It’s POSSIBLE to have each person after the first get nobs (A - Ace of clubs, B- Jack of clubs, C - Jack of clubs, D - Jack of clubs <and 31 for 2 more points>)
- The game is over when one person gets 30 points ( on my board - an up/down not a race track style - that’s the top of the lane. )

If you have any questions, email me and I’ll be glad to explain the rules a bit better. If you come up with any variations or other similar games (war euchre for instance), I’ll be glad to post them.

Have fun!

June 13, 2003
That's John "Mr. CCNA" Vincent to you!

Well I passed the CCNA.

Let me just tell you that the test is NOT easy. There is ALOT of stuff to know. It helps that I've been "doing" for the past 5+ years but it doesn't make it any easier.

I don't always remember commands off the top of my head and I have to occasionally look some things up.

Having said that, here are my tips for the CCNA.

1) Know subnetting like the back of your hand. I spent time to make sure I UNDERSTOOD it and then went back and made my own memorization charts. Know why the shortcuts work.

2) Routing is pretty important. I had more routing than switching questions.

3) Know subnetting.

4) Know IOS. The labs are not that easy. You have some access to "sh?" and "?" commands but you the labs are pretty involved. You don't want to waste time typing "encap?" "encapsulation ?" "encapsulation frame-relay ?" and on and on. The tab key DOES not work. Shortcuts such as "sh int e 0" are valid though. You aren't penalized for spelling mistakes either. I think the way the labs work are by comparing the configs left after you get done with one that is correct.

5) Know subnetting.

That's all I remember. Honestly. When I took my Microsoft tests, they weren't this hard (although I understand that they've gotten more real world and less regugitation). Even if you pass with a low score (hard to get since passing is 849/1000), you'll feel like you earned it. If you KNOW there are some areas that you feel like you performed not so well on, take a week off and then go back and read them after you pass the test. That is, if you feel like the CCNA is more than just a path to a higher paycheck. For me, it was a way to show employers that I know what I'm talking about.

Oh yeah, since I've been a network administrator for several years now, I just purchased the self-study guide for the 640-607 and the flash card kit. I'd also suggest getting a Safari Account. Might I suggest you check out "Cisco® CCNA® Exam #640-607 Certification Guide" and print the "Foundation Summary" for each chapter. You can keep these around for the rest of your career as a quick ref. Also print out the Chapter 12 scenarios and black out the answers. Sit down and do them and then compare with the correct answers. These were probably the greatest help to me as it's a set of three scenarios that covers everything in a real world style example.

Good luck to anyone else taking it. If I get the job I've been working on, I plan on taking the ENE in several months. since they use Extreme stuff in-house.

Thomas Friedman spells it out

Welcome to AJC!

Let me say this. Recently I've been feeling like I bought into some sort of lie.

I was the first to admit that I never felt threatened by Iraq. My thought was that I trusted Colin Powell more than the UN.

After all is said and done, I pick up a periodical a few weeks ago at Barnes and Noble while getting a few study guides for the CCNA.

In this magazine, there was an article that disected the GWB speech before we went to war (the 48 hours speech). After various parts, the writer would add his commentary. He made some interesting points. Much of it centered around the LP policy of foriegn involvement. His point was that while Saddam was an asshole (my words) and deserved whatever came to him, the information regarding WOMDs was inaccurate and that even if it were valid, Iraq is a soveriegn state and that we had no right to attack them.

Let me make a few points.

I don't care how many people think that the 9-11 attackers came from Iraq. That proves nothing except the stupidity of the uneducated populace.

I don't care that some people are calling the war illegal. My interpretation is that the original UN resolution gave any member of the security council the right to enforce disarmament. I don't like the UN one bit but they did make the rule.

Things in the world are alot closer now. Someone over in the middle east can pose a threat to our national security and not be on our shores. 9-11 itself proved that the definition of a threat has grown.

Either way, while Friedman (a writer who I love reading and usually agree with) says that he would have accepted that we had to attack Iraq for stability in the Middle East, I don't think I would have. I just wish I hadn't bought into the bull. I'm too smart for that.
